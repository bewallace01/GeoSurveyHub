## 1. THE OPPORTUNITY

The surveying industry is in a structural talent shortage. At Geo Week 2026,
workforce discussions pointed to demand for geospatial talent in North America
expanding far faster than qualified graduates are entering the field. This isn't
a shrinking industry — it's one where qualified, licensed operators can build
profitable businesses with the right preparation.

Market size context:
- Global GIS market: $12.62B in 2026, growing to $26.48B by 2035 (8.59% CAGR)
- Geospatial analytics market: $97.45B in 2026, projected $309.84B by 2034
- The surveying segment is one of the fastest-growing GIS application areas
- North America holds the largest regional share at ~35%

Why now is a good time:
- Drone LiDAR has democratized aerial survey — a two-person firm can now do
  what required a helicopter and a large crew 10 years ago
- AI-assisted classification cuts processing time from days to hours
- Remote work + cloud-native software reduces overhead dramatically
- Construction activity, infrastructure spending, and real estate development
  all drive consistent surveying demand

## 2. WHAT IT TAKES — THE HONEST ANSWER

Starting a surveying company is a two-track challenge:
  Track 1: Getting licensed (technical/professional path)
  Track 2: Building a business (legal/financial/operational path)

You need BOTH. A licensed surveyor who can't manage a business will struggle.
A savvy businessperson without a licensed surveyor cannot legally operate.

## 3. LICENSING — THE COMPLETE PATH

### Federal Framework (applies in all 50 states)
All surveying licensing in the US flows through NCEES — the National Council
of Examiners for Engineering and Surveying. NCEES administers the standardized
exams that every state requires.

You cannot legally practice surveying, sign survey documents, or operate a
surveying firm without a Professional Surveyor (PS) license in the state
where work is performed. Each state licenses independently — you must hold
a license in every state where you practice.

### The Standard Path to PS Licensure

STEP 1: Education
  - Minimum: Bachelor's degree from an ABET-accredited surveying program
    (surveying, geomatics, surveying engineering, or surveying engineering technology)
  - Some states accept a high school diploma + extended experience, but this path
    is slower and increasingly uncommon for new entrants
  - ABET-accredited programs found at abet.org
  - Relevant fields: Geomatics, Civil Engineering (with surveying focus),
    Geography with technical surveying coursework

STEP 2: Fundamentals of Surveying (FS) Exam
  - Administered by NCEES
  - Available to students in their final year or graduates of ABET programs
  - Tests breadth of surveying principles: mathematics, measurements,
    coordinate systems, boundary law, photogrammetry, GIS concepts
  - Passing makes you a Surveyor-in-Training (SIT) — you can work under
    a licensed PS but cannot sign documents independently
  - Can be retaken if failed (waiting period varies by state)

STEP 3: Supervised Experience
  - Most states require 4 years of progressive experience under a licensed PS
  - California requires 6 years
  - Experience must be documented and verifiable
  - Quality matters: experience should include both field and office components
  - Typical breakdown: boundary surveys, topographic surveys, construction
    stakeout, research, drafting/CAD work

STEP 4: Principles and Practice of Surveying (PS) Exam
  - Administered by NCEES
  - Tests competency in professional practice: boundary law, surveying methods,
    professional ethics, regulations, calculations
  - More advanced than the FS exam — pass rates vary by state

STEP 5: State-Specific Exam
  - Most states require an additional state exam after passing the PS
  - Tests knowledge of state-specific laws, standards, and regulations
  - North Carolina exam administered 4x per year (Feb, May, Aug, Nov)
  - Some neighboring-state reciprocity agreements allow waiver of this exam

### License Reciprocity
Once licensed in one state, most states offer reciprocity — allowing you to
obtain a license in an additional state without re-taking the full exam path,
provided you meet their education and experience requirements. This is how
multi-state firms operate. Check NCEES's NICET database for state-by-state
reciprocity details.

### Timeline Reality Check
The fastest realistic path:
  - 4-year ABET bachelor's degree
  - Pass FS exam in final year
  - 4 years supervised experience (total ~8 years from starting college)
  - Pass PS exam + state exam
  - Apply for license
Most new PS licensees are in their late 20s to early 30s. Some career-changers
pursue this path in their 30s-40s after prior careers in construction or engineering.

## 4. OPTIONAL CERTIFICATIONS (enhance credibility and billings)

### ALTA/NSPS Certification
For performing commercial boundary surveys that meet American Land Title
Association standards. Required for commercial real estate transactions.
Commands significantly higher fees than standard boundary surveys.

### Certified Survey Technician (CST) Program
NSPS certification program with 5 levels — from field party chief basics
through senior technician. Levels 3-5 cover specific disciplines including
hydrography, photogrammetry, and GIS. Good for staff development.

### FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate
Required to operate commercial drones in the US (for survey drone work).
Written exam, no practical test. ~$160 exam fee. Valid for 2 years.
Every surveying firm doing aerial work needs this.

### ASPRS Certification
American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing offers certifications
in photogrammetry, remote sensing, GIS/LIS, and lidar. Strong credibility
signal for government contracts and larger project work.

## 5. BUSINESS FORMATION

Starting a licensed surveying business requires BOTH:
  1. The individual PS license (for the person signing surveys)
  2. Business entity registration

### Business Entity Options

Sole Proprietorship:
  - Simplest structure
  - You and the business are legally the same entity
  - No liability separation — personal assets at risk
  - Suitable only for very small, low-risk solo operations
  - Not recommended for a serious surveying business

LLC (Limited Liability Company):
  - Most common structure for small surveying firms
  - Separates personal and business liability
  - Pass-through taxation (profits taxed on personal return)
  - Relatively easy to form ($50–$500 in most states)
  - Can have multiple members/owners
  - Recommended for most new surveying businesses

Professional Corporation (PC) or Professional LLC (PLLC):
  - Required in some states for licensed professional practices
  - Provides corporate structure with professional practice provisions
  - Check your state's requirements — some states specifically require PLLC
    or PC for surveying practices
  - Slightly more complex to form and maintain

S-Corporation:
  - More complex tax structure
  - Can reduce self-employment taxes at higher income levels
  - Worth considering once annual revenue exceeds ~$80,000
  - Requires payroll + reasonable salary to owner

### Required Registrations (typical, varies by state)
  - State business entity registration ($50–$500)
  - Business license from city/county (varies)
  - Employer Identification Number (EIN) from IRS — free, apply at irs.gov
  - State surveying firm registration (separate from individual license in many states)
  - Professional Liability (E&O) insurance — required for most contracts
  - General Liability insurance — required for most commercial work
  - Workers' Compensation insurance — required if you have employees

## 6. INSURANCE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

Surveying involves signing legal documents that can affect property values,
construction projects, and real estate transactions. Errors can cost clients
millions. Insurance is not optional.

Professional Liability / Errors & Omissions (E&O):
  - Covers claims arising from errors, omissions, or negligence in surveying work
  - Typical coverage: $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate for a small firm
  - Annual premium: $2,000–$8,000 depending on revenue and project types
  - Some clients and government contracts require minimum coverage levels

General Liability:
  - Covers bodily injury and property damage during field operations
  - Typical coverage: $1M per occurrence
  - Annual premium: $800–$2,500
  - Required by most commercial property owners before access is granted

Equipment Insurance:
  - Total stations, GNSS receivers, scanners, drones — these are expensive
  - Covers theft, accidental damage, and loss in the field
  - Inland marine / equipment floater policies: $500–$2,000/year
  - Check manufacturer warranties and consider gap coverage

Commercial Auto:
  - If using a vehicle for business: personal auto policy does NOT cover commercial use
  - Required if driving to job sites in a company vehicle
  - Annual premium: $1,200–$3,500

Drone Insurance:
  - Required for commercial drone operations (FAA Part 107)
  - Hull coverage (physical drone) + liability coverage
  - Annual premium: $500–$1,500 per drone

## 7. STARTUP COSTS — REALISTIC BUDGET RANGES

### Solo / Minimal Operation (boundary surveys, residential work)
Equipment:
  - Total station (used/mid-range): $8,000–$15,000
  - RTK GNSS rover kit (Emlid Reach RS3 or similar): $3,000–$6,000
  - Field computer/data collector: $2,500–$4,000
  - Vehicle (used pickup): $20,000–$35,000
  - Basic hand tools, safety equipment: $1,000–$2,000
  - Software (CAD + field data): $2,000–$5,000/year

Business setup:
  - LLC formation + state registrations: $500–$1,500
  - E&O insurance (first year): $2,500–$5,000
  - General liability: $1,000–$2,000
  - Website + marketing: $1,500–$3,000
  - Office setup (home office or minimal): $1,000–$3,000

TOTAL SOLO STARTUP: ~$45,000 to $85,000

### Small Crew Operation (2–3 people, broader service offering)
Equipment adds:
  - Drone platform + LiDAR or photogrammetry payload: $15,000–$60,000
  - Second RTK rover: $5,000–$25,000
  - Processing workstation: $3,000–$6,000
  - Crew vehicle (additional): $25,000–$45,000

Staffing:
  - Survey technician salary: $45,000–$65,000/year
  - Workers' comp, payroll taxes: +15–20% of wages

TOTAL SMALL CREW STARTUP: ~$120,000 to $250,000

### Technology-Forward Operation (drone LiDAR focus)
Adds:
  - Professional drone LiDAR system (DJI M350 + L2): $25,000–$45,000
  - LiDAR processing software (LP360, TerraSolid): $3,000–$8,000/year
  - Marketing to differentiate from traditional firms: $5,000–$15,000/year

## 8. REVENUE POTENTIAL

### Pricing by Survey Type (2025–2026 market rates)
Boundary Survey (residential, <1 acre): $800–$2,500
Boundary Survey (rural, 1–10 acres): $1,500–$5,000
Topographic Survey (residential): $1,200–$3,500
ALTA/NSPS Survey (commercial): $2,500–$8,000+
Construction Survey (stakeout, per visit): $600–$2,000
As-Built Survey: $1,200–$4,500
Subdivision Plat: $3,000–$12,000+
Hourly rate: $175–$450/hour depending on market and complexity

### Drone Survey Premium Pricing
Drone Topographic (per 100 acres, open terrain): $2,500–$6,000
Drone LiDAR (per 100 acres, forested): $5,000–$15,000
Orthophoto deliverable: $1,500–$4,000/project
As-Built Progress Monitoring (construction, per visit): $2,000–$5,000

### Revenue Scenarios

Solo Licensed Surveyor (Year 1–2):
  - 3–4 projects/week × $1,500 average → $225,000–$300,000 gross revenue
  - After overhead, insurance, equipment depreciation, vehicle costs
  - Net to owner: $65,000–$120,000

Two-person Firm (Year 2–3):
  - Gross revenue: $400,000–$700,000
  - Net profit (15–25% margin): $60,000–$175,000
  - Owner compensation typically comes from a combination of profit + salary

Five-person Established Firm:
  - Gross revenue: $1,000,000–$2,500,000
  - Well-run firms achieve 25–30%+ net profit margin
  - At $1M revenue and 25% margin: $250,000 net profit
  - Owner compensation: $150,000–$300,000+ depending on structure

Technology-Differentiated Firm (drone LiDAR, AI processing):
  - Premium pricing for specialized services
  - Drone LiDAR operator can charge $8,000–$20,000/day for airborne surveys
  - Firms specializing in corridor mapping or infrastructure can bill
    $5,000–$15,000/day for mobile mapping system operations
  - Niche specialization (historic preservation, mining, utilities) commands
    20–40% higher rates than generalist competitors

### Profit Margin Benchmarks
Industry average (small firms): 15–20% net
Well-managed firms: 25–30% net
Top-performing specialized firms: 30%+ net
Key driver: firms that track billable utilization and price by value (not just
hours) consistently outperform those that guess.

## 9. CLIENT ACQUISITION

### Who hires surveyors?
- Real estate attorneys and title companies (boundary, ALTA surveys)
- Land developers and homebuilders (subdivision, topographic, construction)
- Municipalities and DOTs (right-of-way, corridor, infrastructure surveys)
- Engineering firms (subcontract field survey work)
- Construction general contractors (as-built, stakeout)
- Homeowners (boundary disputes, fence lines, property sales)
- Federal agencies (USACE, USGS, DOT — large government contracts)
- Environmental firms (wetland delineation, floodplain surveys)
- Utility companies (corridor, asset inventory)
- Mining operations (volumetric, progress surveys)

### Where to find early clients
- Local title companies and real estate attorneys: high-volume repeat business
- Your county/city GIS and planning department: government survey work
- Engineering firms: subcontracting relationships
- Online directories: NSPS member directory, local chamber of commerce
- LinkedIn: engineering, construction, and development professionals
- Local building permit office: developers filing permits need surveys

### Pricing to win early
- First 6 months: price competitively to build relationships and portfolio
- Do NOT undercut wildly — it devalues the profession and attracts difficult clients
- Focus on speed and responsiveness: surveyors who call back fast and deliver
  on time earn repeat business faster than those who compete on price alone

## 10. YEAR ONE ROADMAP

Month 1–3: Foundation
  □ Pass PS exam (if not already licensed) and obtain state license
  □ Form LLC/PLLC and register with state
  □ Obtain E&O and general liability insurance
  □ Get FAA Part 107 if doing drone work
  □ Purchase minimum viable equipment
  □ Set up basic bookkeeping (QuickBooks or similar)

Month 3–6: First Revenue
  □ Contact 10 title companies and introduce your firm
  □ Register with your county as a minority/small/veteran-owned business if applicable
  □ Complete first 5–10 projects, deliver on time, ask for referrals
  □ Build a basic website with your services and contact info
  □ Price projects: never lower than 2.5× your direct cost

Month 6–12: Growth
  □ Track billable utilization weekly (target >70% of working hours billable)
  □ Hire first part-time or full-time technician when billable hours max out
  □ Raise rates 10–20% after establishing a client base
  □ Pursue one government or engineering firm relationship for steady work
  □ Add one new service capability (e.g., drone photogrammetry)

Year 2–3: Scale
  □ Specialize: identify the 2–3 service types where you earn the best margins
  □ Add equipment that expands those services
  □ Pursue ALTA certification for commercial survey work
  □ Consider expanding to adjacent state (reciprocity license)
  □ Build recurring revenue through annual monitoring or maintenance contracts

## 11. COMMON MISTAKES (AVOID THESE)

Underpricing: The most common fatal mistake. Survey work is skilled,
licensed, and liability-bearing. Price accordingly. Use 3× direct cost
as a floor, not a target.

Ignoring overhead: Most new firms underestimate overhead by 20–30%.
Equipment depreciation, insurance, vehicle costs, and software eat more
than expected.

Working without E&O insurance: One boundary error on a commercial project
can generate a claim that exceeds your entire year's revenue. Never work
uninsured.

Practicing across state lines without a license: Illegal and a license
termination risk. Always verify you hold a current license in the state
where work is performed.

Taking on work outside your competence: A residential boundary surveyor
should not immediately tackle an ALTA commercial survey or LiDAR corridor
project without proper training and experience. Know your scope.

No written contracts: Every project needs a written scope and fee agreement.
Scope creep without a change order mechanism destroys margins.

## 12. KEY ORGANIZATIONS AND RESOURCES

NCEES (ncees.org): National exam and licensing body. Check state-specific
requirements, exam schedules, and reciprocity agreements.

NSPS (nsps.us.com): National Society of Professional Surveyors. Industry
association, advocacy, continuing education, and the CST certification program.

ASPRS (asprs.org): American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing.
Photogrammetry and remote sensing certification and standards.

Your State Association: Every state has a professional surveying association
(e.g., NCSPS in North Carolina, CLSA in California). Join early — these
relationships lead to referrals and mentorship.

ACSM (acsm.net): American Congress on Surveying and Mapping. Policy,
education, and advocacy.

Geo Week (geo-week.com): The primary annual industry conference. Attend
once a year to stay current on technology and build vendor relationships.

SBA Small Business Administration (sba.gov): Business formation guidance,
loan programs, and free business mentoring (SCORE).
